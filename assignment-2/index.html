Owlflight by Mercedes Lackey & Larry Dixon

Synopsis

New York Times-bestselling author: Take flight with the first adventure in the Owl Mage trilogy—an epic fantasy full of action, gryphons, and an elf-like race that forms magical bonds with birds!
After fleeing into the forest when barbarians attack his village, a young orphan encounters the Hawkbrothers—and finds his place among them.
Darian’s parents had been hunters who worked in the Pelagiris forest, trapping the bizarre change-creatures which had been created by the mage-storms, and selling their fantastic hides. But Darian had not accompanied them on their last expedition into the Pelagiris—a hunt from which they never returned.
Now Darian is apprenticed to Wizard Justyn, a kindly old man who insists that Darian has “talent.” But Darian, grieving over his parents, has no interest in magic—and instead of studying, finds solace in the forest, where can hide among the huge trees and mourn in privacy.
It is from this secret retreat on the edge of the Pelagiris that Darian sees an army of northern barbarians sack and burn his village. Alone and helpless, Darian flees into the deep forest. But unbeknownst to him, the Hawkbrothers—an old, magical, and elf-like race—dwell in the ancient woods, and his flight will lead him on a path of discovery which neither Justyn nor Darian’s parents could have ever predicted.

Published by DAW Oct. 1, 1998.

Review by Klaus Kreitz

Brilliant, Fascinating, Unique

This is book one of a trilogy (Owl Flight; Owl Sight; Owl Knight), so will review it as an entire set. I enjoyed it. I particularly appreciate the way in which Lackey introduces a young character and then allows us to experience that young character's journey into adulthood. I purchased the first volume because of the title (I collect owl figurines!) and was pleasantly surprised at how much I enjoyed Owl Flight, and how anxiously I awaited the publication of the subsequent books in the series, which continued the joyous arc of our hero's journey. Anyone who had read Mercedes Lackey's Valdemar series will understand the detailed descriptions with which she fills her narratives, and how deftly she paints a picture, with a few words, of each location. More so, there is a passion in her writing which seems to make the characters come to life, and in this series, one can almost hear the owl-wings beating. Definitely a worthy addition to the Valdemar series, that keeps on giving joy to this reader.
